How to Study for AP Psychology

The exam is roughly 65% application, not recall. Memorizing definitions isn't enough — you have to practice applying concepts to scenarios and data. Here's how to do that efficiently.

Techniques that actually work

Cognitive psychology (which you're studying in Unit 2!) tells us what makes studying stick. Use these:

  • Retrieval practice. Testing yourself is far more effective than re-reading. That's why this site is built around questions with explanations. Try to recall before you peek.
  • Spaced practice. Short sessions across many days beat one long cram. The daily-streak feature nudges you to come back — use it.
  • Interleaving. Mix topics rather than blocking one at a time. After a unit, do a shuffled set that spans its topics (turn on "Shuffle").
  • Elaboration. Ask "why" and "how does this connect?" When you read an explanation, put the misconception into your own words.
  • Concrete examples. For every concept, invent your own real-world scenario. The exam will give you unfamiliar scenarios — practice generating them.

Classic distractor traps

The exam's wrong answers are engineered from real student misconceptions. If you can articulate each of these distinctions, you'll gain points:

  • Correlation vs. causation. A correlation (even a strong one) never establishes cause. Only a well-controlled experiment with random assignment supports causal claims.
  • Pavlov vs. Skinner. Classical conditioning (Pavlov) = associating two stimuli; the behavior is reflexive/involuntary. Operant (Skinner) = behavior changed by its consequences; the behavior is voluntary.
  • Negative reinforcement vs. punishment. Negative reinforcement removes something aversive to increase a behavior. Punishment decreases a behavior. "Negative" means "take away," not "bad."
  • Piaget vs. Erikson. Piaget = cognitive stages (how thinking develops). Erikson = psychosocial stages (identity/relationships across the lifespan).
  • Proactive vs. retroactive interference. Proactive = old learning blocks new. Retroactive = new learning blocks old. ("Pro" = forward in time from the old memory.)
  • Independent vs. dependent variable. The independent variable is manipulated; the dependent variable is measured (it "depends" on the IV).
